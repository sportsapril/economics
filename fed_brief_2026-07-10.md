## 🎙️ Sentiment Read

Net assessment: **hawkish-leaning but genuinely split**, with communications style itself becoming a variable. The 9-8-1 minutes split, the upward dot-plot revision, and the unhedged "deliver price stability" line all point toward a Committee more willing to tolerate hawkish framing than under the prior chair. Warsh has also explicitly broken from Powell-era practice by declining to pre-signal the Committee's next move — at the ECB's Sintra forum (early July) he said only that "prices are too high" and that officials would have "a good debate" at the July meeting, offering no directional hint. That contrasts somewhat with Governor Waller's July 6 remarks in Rome, where he called forward guidance "a valuable tool" when used flexibly — a modest internal difference in communications philosophy, not necessarily in the rate-path view.

Two complicating, non-monetary-policy threads worth flagging: (1) President Trump said on July 2 that Warsh faces a Fed board that is "a little bit hostile" and "wants to do the wrong thing" — commentary that cuts against, not for, market perceptions of Fed independence, since it implies the White House expects Warsh to lean dovish; Warsh has said he's made no promises to anyone on the rate path. (2) The July 9 task-force announcement, staffed with eminent, largely apolitical figures (Mervyn King, Raghuram Rajan, Thomas Sargent, Greg Mankiw, among others), reads as an institution-building move — plausibly an effort by Warsh to bank analytical credibility and process legitimacy at a moment when his independence is under public scrutiny. This is our interpretation, not a stated Fed rationale.
