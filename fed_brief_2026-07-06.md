Hawkish, but with real tension between the data and the message. Specifics:
- The June 17 statement's brevity (~130 words vs. 300+ previously) and the Fed's stated move away from traditional forward guidance is itself a sentiment signal — Warsh wants the Fed judged meeting-by-meeting on data, not on a pre-committed path. That makes each incoming inflation and jobs print more market-moving than under the Powell-era communication style.
- Warsh's Sintra remarks ("prices are too high," markets expecting tolerance above 2% "would be disappointed") are unambiguously hawkish rhetoric, delivered while core PCE is running at 3.4% YoY — well above target.
- The June 17 Summary of Economic Projections (dot plot) backs this up with numbers: the median fed-funds projection for end-2026 rose to 3.8% from 3.4% in March — a level above the current 3.50-3.75% range's midpoint, meaning the median FOMC participant now sees a hike, not a cut, by year-end. Of 18 officials, one wanted a cumulative 75bp of hikes for the rest of 2026, five wanted 50bp, three wanted 25bp, eight favored no change, and one still wanted a 25bp cut — a split committee, but the center of gravity moved firmly toward tightening versus March. [Fed SEP materials, June 17, 2026](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcprojtabl20260617.htm)
- Against that, June payrolls at just 57k (with the unemployment-rate decline driven by shrinking labor-force participation, not stronger hiring) is a soft, labor-market-cooling data point that markets read as reducing the odds and urgency of a hike, per CME FedWatch's post-payrolls repricing.
- Net effect: the Fed is talking hawkish on inflation while the real economy sends a more mixed, softening signal. Markets responded by trimming rate-hike odds rather than eliminating them — the July hike probability fell but is still a live scenario (~20-25%), not a rounding error.
