## 🎙️ Sentiment Read

**Hawkish, and more hawkish than markets expected when Kevin Warsh took over as Fed chair on May 22, 2026.** Warsh — a self-described advocate of "regime change" at the Fed before his confirmation — has used his first weeks in the chair to lean into inflation-fighting rhetoric rather than the rate-cutting path some investors anticipated from a Trump appointee. His July 14 testimony ("prices are too high," "not satisfied" with the pace of disinflation) and June 17 post-meeting message that the Committee would be "unambiguous and unanimous" on price stability both signal a Fed unwilling to look past above-target inflation, even with payroll growth clearly slowing (57,000 jobs in June) — [CNBC, "Markets are set for a much more hawkish Warsh Fed than expected," June 18](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/18/markets-are-set-for-a-much-more-hawkish-warsh-fed-than-expected.html); [Federal Reserve testimony, July 14](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/testimony/warsh20260714a.htm).

The June 17 FOMC statement itself flagged that inflation "remains elevated relative to the Committee's 2 percent goal, in part reflecting supply shocks... including energy" tied to the conflict in the Middle East — language that has aged into a live risk now that the Strait of Hormuz tensions have pushed oil back above $75–78/barrel. That combination (a hawkish new chair + an energy-driven inflation shock + still-elevated core PCE) is why rate-hike odds, not cut odds, have become the live debate this cycle, even as the labor market cools.
