## 🎙️ Sentiment Read

The Fed's posture has turned meaningfully more hawkish since Warsh took over as chair in May, anchored to specifics rather than tone alone:

- The June 17 post-meeting statement was cut to roughly a third of its prior typical length and dropped language signaling a bias toward future cuts — a deliberate Warsh-driven shift toward less forward guidance, per [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/17/fed-interest-rate-decision-june-2026.html).
- The Summary of Economic Projections released at that meeting showed the median year-end-2026 rate projection rising to 3.8% from 3.4% in March — a shift from an implied cut path to a hike-leaning one — with 9 of 18 officials now projecting a higher rate by year-end.
- The July 8 minutes showed "a few" officials making the case for a hike at the June meeting itself, and flagged three concurrent inflationary channels: tariffs, Iran-war-linked energy costs, and — newly — AI infrastructure demand pushing up prices for tech products and electricity.
- Elevated inflation (3.8% CPI, 3.4% core PCE, both multi-year highs) alongside a still-solid labor market (4.3% unemployment) gives the Committee room to prioritize inflation-fighting over growth support, consistent with Warsh's stated framework.

Net read: **hawkish drift, not yet a hike**. The bar for a July 29 hike appears meaningfully lower than it did a few months ago, but market pricing (~70% hold, per CME FedWatch) still favors no change at the very next meeting.

- **Yield-curve reaction:** The curve remains positively sloped (2s10s around +35 to +38bp as of July 9–10), a shift from the inverted curve of 2022–2024. - **No consensus "recession call":** None of the reputable sources reviewed are forecasting an imminent recession; the yield curve is not inverted (a traditional recession signal), though the inflation overshoot and tighter-for-longer narrative are being treated as headwinds rather than a downturn trigger.
