## 🏛️ What's New From the Fed

**FOMC decision (June 16–17 meeting; statement released June 17, 2:00 p.m. EDT).** The Committee held the target range at **3-1/2 to 3-3/4 percent** by a **12–0 vote**. The statement was deliberately rewritten — "a bit shorter, a bit simpler," in Warsh's words — and dropped prior language signaling a bias toward future cuts. Key new line: *"Inflation remains elevated relative to the Committee's 2 percent goal, in part reflecting supply shocks… The Committee will deliver price stability."* It also flagged "elevated uncertainty that owes, in part, to the conflict in the Middle East" while describing activity as "expanding at a solid pace."
Source: Federal Reserve — https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260617a.htm

**Summary of Economic Projections / dot plot — the main market mover.** The median projection for the fed funds rate at end-2026 rose to **3.8%, up from 3.4%** in the March SEP — implying the Committee now sees at least one **hike** as likely this year. Participants were split: roughly **9 of 18 saw rates finishing 2026 above the current range**, about 8 saw no change, and 1 saw a cut. Notably, **Warsh abstained from submitting his own dot**, calling the practice "not helpful in the conduct of policy."

**Press conference — Warsh announces five task forces.** In his debut presser, Warsh unveiled five reviews pairing internal staff with outside experts, covering the Fed's **communications, balance sheet, data, productivity & jobs, and the inflation framework**. He singled out a task force to examine the **$6.7 trillion balance sheet**, a structure he has long criticized, and signaled a year-end review of "communication broadly, press conferences, dots, meetings… transcripts, minutes."

## 🎙️ Sentiment Read

**Decidedly hawkish — the most hawkish FOMC signal in some time.** The shift is anchored in specifics, not tone:

1. **The dot plot moved up 40 bp** at the median (3.4% → 3.8% for end-2026), flipping the base case from cuts to a likely hike — the single biggest driver of the market reaction.
2. **The statement was rewritten to remove easing bias** and add an unambiguous commitment: *"The Committee will deliver price stability."*
3. **Warsh's language reinforced it** — repeated emphasis on "price stability" and "inflation is a choice," signaling he may not pursue the cuts many expected from a Trump nominee. His refusal to submit a dot, plus the promised communications review, injects added uncertainty about forward guidance.

The hawkishness is a response to sticky inflation (headline CPI 4.2% y/y, core PCE rising to 2.8%) against a labor market that is, if anything, re-accelerating (strongest three-month payroll run in over two years), giving the Committee room to lean against price pressures.
