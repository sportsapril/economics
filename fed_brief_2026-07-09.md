## 🏛️ What's New From the Fed

**FOMC Minutes (June 16–17, 2026 meeting) — released Wednesday, July 8 at 2:00 p.m. ET.** This is the single new Fed-official development in the last 24 hours; there were no new speeches, testimony, or Beige Book entries since the last cycle.

- **Vote:** All 12 voting members supported holding the target range at 3.50%–3.75% (12–0). The minutes confirm "a few participants" argued there was a case for raising the range at this meeting but backed holding for now.
- **Genuinely split committee on the path forward:** Per the minutes, "many participants indicated that the appropriate level of the federal funds rate would be within or slightly below the current target range at the end of this year. Many other participants, however, assessed that the appropriate level of the federal funds rate would be above the current target range at the end of this year." Most participants sketched two live scenarios — one where inflation cools and holds/cuts are appropriate, another where inflation stays elevated on "strong AI-related demand, the conflict in the Middle East, or the effects of tariffs" and "some policy firming would likely be warranted."
- **Statement rewrite:** The Committee shortened its post-meeting statement and dropped language that had signaled an "easing bias," instead emphasizing that "the Committee will deliver price stability." Several participants said they preferred not to repeat the prior easing-bias language.
- **Inflation framing:** Participants said inflation had "increased further and remained well above the Committee's 2 percent longer-run objective," attributing it to tariff pass-through, Strait of Hormuz-related supply disruption, and "strength in demand for some goods and services stemming from robust AI-related investment." Risks to the inflation outlook were judged "still tilted to the upside." Staff's own inflation forecast was revised higher, and staff continued to see "the possibility that inflation would be more persistent than projected as a salient risk."
- **Dot plot context (from the June 17 SEP, discussed in the minutes):** Nine of 18 projecting officials penciled in at least one 2026 rate hike, versus zero in March — a sharp hawkish reversal. New Chair Kevin Warsh did not submit a projection at all, the first time a sitting chair has withheld a dot since the SEP began in 2012, which is why market attention has concentrated on the minutes as the fullest on-record read of the committee's thinking.
