## 🏛️ What's New From the Fed

**Chair Kevin Warsh at the ECB Forum (Sintra), July 1** — In his first major speech on the global stage, new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh said inflation remains "too high" but that inflation *risks* have declined in recent weeks. He tied the improvement to energy: "energy prices have come down quite substantially" since the U.S. and Iran signed a memorandum of understanding to end their war. He called labor markets "steady" and the demand side of the economy "solid… this is before we see the fruits of AI," expressing openness to the idea that AI could prove deflationary and boost productivity. Notably, he **declined to hint at the July rate decision**. He also described a "new course" for Fed operations, including task forces on data and on how the Fed measures and reacts to inflation, and said he hopes that within 9–12 months the Fed will read the real economy in near-real time.

## 🎙️ Sentiment Read

**Net hawkish, but with an emerging dovish tail risk.** The hard anchor remains the June SEP: the median 2026 dot moved up to **3.8%** (from 3.4% in March), and nine of 18 officials projected the funds rate finishing the year *above* the current range — i.e., the Committee's base case still leans toward one more hike. The June statement kept inflation described as "elevated" and pointed to energy supply shocks.

Warsh's July 1 tone partially softens that. Saying inflation risks "have declined" and crediting falling energy prices (post US–Iran MOU) is incrementally dovish at the margin, and his openness to AI-as-deflationary is a notable framing shift. But he explicitly refused to signal a July move and repeated that prices are "too high," keeping optionality for a hike alive. The market read the balance as hawkish: the FedWatch hold probability fell from ~89% to ~70% through June as the hike tail fattened.
