## 🎙️ Sentiment Read

The policy tone remains more hawkish than at any point in the last several years, but June's jobs data complicates it. Specifics:

- The June 17 FOMC statement explicitly cited "elevated uncertainty that owes, in part, to the conflict in the Middle East" and stated inflation "remains elevated relative to the Committee's 2 percent goal, in part reflecting supply shocks... including energy," closing with "The Committee will deliver price stability" — a notably firmer inflation-fighting line than prior statements that emphasized "maintaining ample reserves" and dual-mandate balance without the explicit price-stability pledge.
- At that same meeting, the Summary of Economic Projections reportedly showed 9 of 19 officials projecting at least one 25bp hike by year-end, with the median end-2026 fed funds projection rising to 3.8% from 3.4% in March (per Fidelity/Yahoo Finance reporting on the SEP) — the first time in years the median dot has pointed toward tightening rather than easing.
- New Chair Kevin Warsh, sworn in May 22, has been characterized in press coverage as emphasizing price stability over labor-market support, consistent with the statement language above.
- Countervailing signal: the weak June payrolls print (+57K, -74K in combined prior-month revisions, falling participation) is the kind of labor-market softening that historically argues against near-term hikes, and desk commentary cited above says it makes a July hike hard to justify even with inflation still running hot.

Net read: this is a rare two-sided setup — inflation and the dot plot argue hawkish, a wobbling labor market argues for patience. The July 28–29 meeting is live for debate but a hold looks like the more likely near-term outcome, with hike risk concentrated in the September–October window if inflation doesn't cool.
