**Governor Christopher Waller — "Monetary Policy at a Crossroads" (July 13, New York Association for Business Economics).** The most consequential item in the last 24 hours. Waller said core PCE inflation has climbed from 3% in December 2025 to 3.4% in May 2026, and warned: "if this upward trend continues, it will be hard to push inflation back toward the Committee's 2 percent goal with monetary policy at its current setting." He explicitly raised the possibility of near-term tightening: "If we get another hot reading on core inflation this week, then the FOMC will need to consider tightening monetary policy in the near term." He also cited AI-driven demand for chips/servers and Middle East-driven energy costs as inflation sources, but noted labor market slack (job vacancy-to-unemployed ratio near 1:1, versus 2:1 in 2022) and anchored inflation expectations (2yr/5yr TIPS breakevens near 2.1%/2.3%) as reasons tightening isn't a foregone conclusion. ## 🎙️ Sentiment Read

**Hawkish tilt, driven by Waller.** Waller's July 13 speech is the clearest data point: he moved from a "watch and wait" stance to explicitly flagging that a hot core-inflation reading this week "will need" a near-term tightening response — unusually direct language for a sitting governor ahead of a CPI print. He anchored this in specifics: core PCE's rise from 3.0% (Dec. 2025) to 3.4% (May 2026), broad-based core services pressure (nearly 70% of categories running above 3% on a 3- and 12-month basis), and 21 straight months of manufacturers reporting rising input costs. He also explicitly rejected the idea that anchored inflation expectations excuse inaction: "Sternly staring at inflation until it melts before our withering gaze is not an option."

Offsetting this, Waller devoted real space to why 2026 differs from 2021–22 (looser labor market, well-anchored TIPS breakevens), leaving him "still" in the camp that holding is the "reasonable" base case — but visibly less confident than in prior speeches. Bowman's remarks were regulatory in nature and carry no monetary-policy signal.

**Market pricing has moved faster than the speeches alone would suggest:** the jump from ~70% hold probability (July 8) to 46.5% hike probability (July 13–14) reflects oil-driven cost-push fears (Iran conflict, Brent near $85) compounding on top of Waller's tone, not the tone alone.

## 📊 Implications for the Broad Stock Market

- **Rate-sensitive sectors under pressure.** With 2-year yields at their highest since February 2025 and hike odds rising, sectors with high rate sensitivity — housing, REITs, small-caps reliant on floating-rate debt, and long-duration growth/tech — face a less favorable discount-rate backdrop than two weeks ago. This is a mechanical/valuation effect, not a prediction of price direction.
- **Growth vs. value:** A rising-hike-odds environment historically pressures long-duration growth names (which discount distant cash flows) more than value/cyclical names. Commentary from Bloomberg and CNBC (cited above) frames this as the market's live debate this week, not a settled outcome.
- **Yield curve reaction:** The 2s10s spread near +35bp remains positively sloped but has compressed somewhat as the 2-year has risen faster than the 10-year on hike repricing — a pattern consistent with markets pulling forward tightening expectations rather than pricing a growth scare.
- **Energy and inflation-linked plays:** Commentators (CNBC, Bloomberg) note the oil-price surge from the Iran conflict is a direct input into both the inflation story and the equity sector rotation — energy names may see relative support while consumer-discretionary and transport names face margin pressure from higher input costs. This is analyst commentary, not verified sector performance data.
- **Event risk concentrated today:** Analysts widely flag the CPI print (8:30am ET) and Warsh's first congressional testimony (10:00am ET) as the two catalysts most likely to move the hike-probability number materially in either direction before markets can fully digest Waller's remarks. Several outlets (Yahoo Finance, Semafor) describe today as Warsh's highest-scrutiny public moment yet as Fed Chair — commentary, not fact about market reaction.
