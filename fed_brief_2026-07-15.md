## 🏛️ What's New From the Fed

**Chair Warsh's first House testimony (July 14) — pushes back on "mission accomplished" framing.** In his inaugural semiannual Monetary Policy Report testimony before the House Financial Services Committee, Chair Kevin Warsh said the Committee has "no tolerance for persistently elevated inflation" and reaffirmed the June 16–17 decision to hold the target range at 3-1/2 to 3-3/4 percent. Reacting to that same morning's cool CPI print, he told lawmakers: "There might be some that look at this morning's data and say, 'Oh, mission accomplished. Everything is swell.' That is not my view." He also flagged AI-related capital spending as a new, hard-to-model demand channel: "We don't know the extent to which the economy will benefit from the AI buildout." Warsh announced five task forces (communications, balance-sheet policy, data/methodology, productivity and jobs, and inflation frameworks) reviewing how the Fed operates. Source: [Federal Reserve — Testimony by Chairman Warsh](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/testimony/warsh20260714a.htm); [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/07/14/economy/takeaways-kevin-warsh-congressional-testimony).

**June CPI report (July 14) — much cooler than expected.** Headline CPI fell 0.4% in June (consensus was -0.2%), pulling annual inflation to 3.5% from May's 4.2% — the first monthly decline in six years. Core CPI (ex food/energy) was flat for the month against a consensus of +0.2%, taking core YoY to 2.6% from 2.9%. The move was driven almost entirely by energy: the energy index fell 5.7% (largest monthly drop since April 2020) as gasoline fell roughly 10%, reflecting a mid-June ceasefire that had briefly reopened Strait of Hormuz tanker traffic. That ceasefire collapsed July 8, so the report is seen as backward-looking. Source: [BLS CPI release](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.htm) via [Tech Times](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/320507/20260714/stunning-cpi-miss-kills-july-rate-hike-hormuz-puts-september-back-table.htm); [Motley Fool](https://www.fool.com/investing/2026/07/14/inflation-slowed-to-35-in-june-but-how-long-will-it-last-heres-what-investors-need-to-know/).

**Governor Waller's July 13 speech set the trigger the CPI then met.** At the New York Association for Business Economics, Waller said "If we get another hot reading on core inflation this week, then the FOMC will need to consider tightening monetary policy in the near term," describing policy as being at a "crossroads" between three inflationary forces: 2025 tariffs, Middle East energy costs, and AI infrastructure demand. He added a caution against "fighting the last war" on inflation. Tuesday's flat core CPI met his stated threshold for continuing to hold, but he had also flagged that ~70% of core services subcategories are still running above 3% annualized. Source: [Federal Reserve — Speech by Governor Waller](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/waller20260713a.htm) via [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/13/waller-says-fed-shouldnt-fight-the-last-war-on-inflation-but-warns-hikes-still-possible.html).

## 🎙️ Sentiment Read

Net sentiment is **hawkish-leaning but genuinely two-sided**, and more volatile than usual because Warsh has deliberately withdrawn the forward guidance markets used to lean on.

Specific anchors: the June FOMC statement dropped its prior easing-bias language for a terse ~130-word statement focused solely on price stability; the median year-end dot moved up to 3.8% from 3.4% in March (implying one hike is the committee's central case), with 9 of 18 participants projecting at least one increase; and Warsh himself declined to submit a dot at all — an unprecedented step for a sitting chair that he has framed as part of reducing the Fed's reliance on explicit forward guidance. His July 14 testimony reinforced this: emphatic on the inflation commitment ("no tolerance"), explicitly non-committal on the rate path, and explicitly rejecting the idea that one cool CPI print changes the picture.

Working against the hawkish reading: the June CPI cooling was broad (not just energy — core was flat too), and unemployment plus weak payroll growth give the Fed a genuine argument for patience. The complicating factor is geopolitical and forward-looking: the Strait of Hormuz ceasefire that produced June's gasoline-driven disinflation collapsed July 8, and WTI crude was trading near $80/barrel on July 14 — a dynamic the June data cannot capture and that will show up first in the July CPI report (due August 12, after the July FOMC meeting). This is why market pricing for July eased sharply while September hike odds (~60%) barely moved.
